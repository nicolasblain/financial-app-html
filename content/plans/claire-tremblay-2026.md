## Executive Summary

Claire (57) is a senior public servant earning $95,000 gross. She owns her condo outright (housing cost: $620/mo condo fees + tax). She has accumulated $842,000 across registered and non-registered accounts and wants to retire at 60 — three years from now.

Her position is strong but not automatic. The $38,000 gap assumes a 4% withdrawal rate and both CPP at 62 and OAS at 67. Closing it requires either a small spending adjustment or one more year of saving at her current rate.

**Top three recommendations:**
1. Drop the $500k term life policy (no dependants, no mortgage) — saves $2,400/yr.
2. Add $50k critical illness coverage — estimated $1,800/yr, addresses the real risk at her age.
3. Begin RRSP-to-RRIF conversion strategy at 60 to smooth tax brackets through to 72.

### RRSP-to-RRIF Conversion Strategy

| Age | Action | Reason |
|-----|--------|--------|
| 60 | Convert partial RRSP to RRIF, withdraw $28,800/yr | Stay in the $55k–$75k bracket |
| 62 | Reduce RRIF withdrawal by CPP amount | Avoid bracket creep |
| 65 | Pension income splitting eligible (if partnered) | N/A currently |
| 67 | Reduce RRIF by OAS amount, clawback threshold ~$90k | Keep OAS intact |
| 72 | Mandatory RRIF minimums apply | Withdrawal floor rises |

**Recommendation:** Begin conversions at 60 with a target taxable income of $55,000/yr. This smooths the tax bill across a 12-year window instead of concentrating it after 72 when mandatory minimums spike.

## Executive Summary

Martin (36) and Sophie (34) are dual-income earners with a combined gross of $142,000. They rent in Montreal ($1,850/mo) and are considering a first home purchase in the $480,000–$520,000 range. Their current savings rate of 14.2% is solid but will be squeezed by homeownership costs. The primary tension in this plan is **home purchase timing vs. retirement gap**: buying at the top of their range pushes the retirement gap from $145,000 to over $280,000.

**Top three recommendations:**
1. Cap home purchase at $480,000 to preserve the savings rate above 10%.
2. Redirect the $200/mo currently going to non-registered into Sophie's TFSA (room: $34,500).
3. Increase life insurance — current group coverage is insufficient given a future mortgage.

## Retirement

**Target:** Retire at 62 with $55,000/yr in today's dollars.

| Scenario | Projected at 62 | Gap |
|----------|----------------:|----:|
| Base case (current savings, 5% real) | $855,000 | -$145,000 |
| Buy at $480k (savings rate drops to 10.5%) | $720,000 | -$280,000 |
| Buy at $480k + increase savings at 40 | $940,000 | -$60,000 |
| Delay purchase 2 years, save aggressively | $1,020,000 | $20,000 |

The gap assumes a 4% withdrawal rate and CPP/QPP at 65 for both. OAS included at 67.

**Key lever:** If they buy at $480k now, they need to increase total savings by $400/mo starting at age 40 to close the gap. That requires either income growth or spending reduction — both plausible but not guaranteed.

**Recommendation:** Buy in the $480k range. Accept the temporary retirement gap, but build a contractual review at age 40 to confirm savings have been increased. If not, retirement age extends to 64.
